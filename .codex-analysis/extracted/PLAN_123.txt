[P134 | Normal (Web)] ## 3. The Recommendation Engine
[P136 | Normal (Web)] Since this is a **personal, single‑user system**, we can start with a powerful content‑based filter and later add neural collaborative filtering as you collect more feedback.
[P138 | Normal (Web)] ### a) Basic Architecture
[P139 | Normal (Web)] - **User profile**: built from your *likes* (right swipes), *dislikes* (left swipes), and *watches*.
[P140 | Normal (Web)] - **Movie vector**: created by concatenating one‑hot encoded genres, TF‑IDF of plot keywords, normalised director/actor influence.
[P141 | Normal (Web)] - **Scoring**: cosine similarity between your profile vector and each unseen movie vector, with penalties for dislikes.
[P143 | Normal (Web)] Everything runs **in the browser** (using TensorFlow.js or pure NumPy‑like libraries) so no backend is strictly needed. Data can stay in `localStorage` / IndexedDB.
[P145 | Normal (Web)] ### b) Handling Swipes, Blocks, and Watches
[P146 | Normal (Web)] - **Right swipe (like)**: adds the movie’s feature vector to a running weighted average profile.
[P147 | Normal (Web)] - **Left swipe (dislike)**: subtracts a fraction of its vector from the profile (negative feedback) and **keeps it out of future feeds** (unless you explicitly reset). This is your “block” action.
[P148 | Normal (Web)] - **Long press / “Block forever”**: a stronger block. The movie is permanently added to a blocklist, never to appear again.
[P149 | Normal (Web)] - **Watched tracking**: when you click “Watch now” and the JioHotstar page opens, your app records `watched: true` + timestamp. Watched items are filtered out by default (you can toggle “show watched” if needed).
[P151 | Normal (Web)] Every time you refresh the page, the system re‑ranks the filtered catalogue using your latest profile.
[P153 | Normal (Web)] ### c) Upgrading to a Neural Network
[P154 | Normal (Web)] Once you have >100 interactions, you can train a **two‑tower model** directly in the browser:
[P155 | Normal (Web)] - **User tower** takes your interaction history (sequence of likes/dislikes) and outputs a user embedding.
[P156 | Normal (Web)] - **Movie tower** takes movie features and outputs a movie embedding.
[P157 | Normal (Web)] - **Similarity** = dot product of embeddings → score for ranking.
[P158 | Normal (Web)] - Loss: contrastive loss (liked pairs should have high similarity; disliked low).
[P160 | Normal (Web)] This can be implemented with TensorFlow.js and trained in a few seconds on your local data. It gives a noticeable lift over pure content‑based methods because it learns non‑linear interactions (e.g., “I like thrillers but only if they star Actor X”).
[P162 | Normal (Web)] ### d) Keeping the Feed “Fresh”
[P163 | Normal (Web)] You can introduce **exploration**:
[P164 | Normal (Web)] - After every block/removal, the system automatically surfaces titles from less‑seen genres or from your “watchlist” (if you maintain one).
[P165 | Normal (Web)] - Use a multi‑armed bandit (ε‑greedy) to occasionally insert a random but high‑rated movie, so the feed never feels stale.

There's no official JioHotstar API, and I'd skip trying to reverse-engineer one. Here's what I found and how I'd actually build this.
[P265 | Normal (Web)] The API question, settled. JioHotstar has no public developer API. Developers occasionally stumble onto undocumented internal endpoints by poking around the app's network traffic, and third-party services sell scraped JioHotstar catalog data — but both of those are exactly the "bypass" territory you said you want to avoid. It's not hacking in the security-exploit sense (you'd just be replaying calls your own logged-in session makes), but it's a clear Terms-of-Service violation and a real account-ban risk. Skip it — you don't need it.
[P266 | Normal (Web)] The legitimate catalog source: TMDB. TMDB's watch-provider data is licensed from JustWatch, and its API exposes a /watch/providers endpoint per movie or show, filterable by region. The useful part for you: JustWatch already tracks JioHotstar as a provider for India, and TMDB has its own JioHotstar network page listing what's currently streaming there. So discover/movie?with_watch_providers={jiohotstar_id}&watch_region=IN gets you a reasonably fresh, legitimately-sourced "what's on JioHotstar right now" list — no scraping needed. Pair it with OMDb for IMDb-specific fields: OMDb's free tier gives 1,000 requests/day versus TMDB's free 40-requests-per-10-seconds with no daily cap, and IMDb's own official data is licensed through AWS Data Exchange — priced for enterprises, not side projects. The 91mobiles page you linked is actually built the same way: it's a filterable catalog of over 4,300 JioHotstar movies and 1,600+ shows with genre, language, cast, and IMDb-rating filters — useful for spot-checking, but it's someone else's site with no API of its own, so TMDB/OMDb stay your primary feed.
[P267 | Normal (Web)] On the streaming backend you asked about — worth knowing, but it's a dead end for this project. JioHotstar runs on AWS with Kubernetes (EKS) orchestrating its microservices, content delivered through CDNs like Akamai, Cloudflare, and CloudFront with adaptive bitrate streaming, and DRM protecting the actual video from piracy. That whole stack is about delivering video bits fast and securely — it's a separate system from the catalog/browse layer you're actually trying to curate. You never need to touch it.
[P268 | Normal (Web)] So here's the shape of the thing:The key insight: don't try to rebuild JioHotstar's homepage from scratch. You'd never get a clean, fully-current feed of "everything on the platform right now" any other way — even TMDB's data lags by hours to days. Instead, build a lightweight browser extension (Manifest V3, just a content script) that runs inside your own logged-in JioHotstar tab. It reads the already-rendered DOM — the movie tiles your browser legitimately requested and received — and:
[P269 | Normal (Web)] hides any tile matching a title on your permanent block-list (display: none, same trick ad blockers and the "Unhook" extension for YouTube use)
[P270 | Normal (Web)] skips anything in your watched-list
[P271 | Normal (Web)] injects a swipe-card layer over (or instead of) JioHotstar's own recommendation rail, populated from your backend's ranked candidate list
[P272 | Normal (Web)] This never touches JioHotstar's API or DRM-protected video — it's pure client-side presentation filtering on a page you're already authorized to view, which is the part of "don't bypass security" you're right to care about. The only maintenance cost: JioHotstar's frontend will change its DOM/class structure occasionally (it's a React SPA), so your selectors will need the odd tune-up. That's a minor annoyance, not a security problem.
[P273 | Normal (Web)] The recommendation engine — three layers, which is basically the "fresh idea" you're after:
[P274 | Normal (Web)] Embedding similarity (the neural-net layer you mentioned). Embed each movie's title+genre+plot+cast with a pretrained sentence-transformer, average the embeddings of everything you've liked into a "taste vector," rank candidates by cosine similarity. Cheap, works from day one, no training required — the "neural network" here is the pretrained encoder.
[P275 | Normal (Web)] A small trainable preference model. Once you've got 50+ swipes, train a lightweight classifier (logistic regression or a 2-layer MLP) on features like genre, embedding, runtime, era, language, IMDb rating → predicted like-probability. Retrain incrementally as swipes accumulate. This is the part that actually learns your taste shape rather than just nearest-neighbor matching.
[P276 | Normal (Web)] An LLM agent as taste-profile keeper and re-ranker. This is the genuinely current direction — recent recommender-system research frames this explicitly as an LLM acting as the reasoning core, using retrieval as a tool, backed by layered memory that distills general preferences from interaction history over time. Concretely: periodically have Claude read your swipe history and write a short natural-language taste profile ("likes slow-burn thrillers, dislikes franchise CGI, gravitates to strong ensemble casts"). At request time, feed the top candidates from layers 1–2 plus that profile to an agent that re-ranks, injects a bit of deliberate exploration so you don't get stuck in a rut, and writes a one-line "why this" for each pick. Given you already work with LangGraph, this slots in naturally as one more node in a graph — retrieve → score → agent-rerank → return.
[P277 | Normal (Web)] Permanent hide, watched-tracking, and "fresh on every refresh" are all just table design, not hard problems: a hidden flag (never shown again, full stop), a watched flag (excluded from candidates, optionally fed back as a positive signal), and a recently_shown log with a cooldown window so the same titles don't repeat call after call — combined with the exploration slice in layer 3, refreshing won't feel stale even with a finite catalog.
[P278 | Normal (Web)] Stack-wise, given what you're comfortable with: FastAPI backend, Postgres with pgvector (or SQLite if you want to keep it light) for the embeddings, a background job that syncs from TMDB/OMDb on a schedule, and a thin LangGraph node for the agent re-rank. The extension itself is just vanilla JS/TS with a small React-based swipe-card overlay if you want Framer-Motion-style swipe physics.
